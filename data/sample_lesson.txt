THE WATER CYCLE

The water cycle, also known as the hydrological cycle, describes the continuous
movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth. It is driven
primarily by solar energy and gravity.

The main stages of the water cycle are:

1. EVAPORATION
Water from oceans, lakes, and rivers is heated by the sun and converted into
water vapour, which rises into the atmosphere. This process is called evaporation.
Plants also release water vapour through a process called transpiration.
Together, these two processes are called evapotranspiration.

2. CONDENSATION
As water vapour rises into the cooler upper atmosphere, it cools and condenses
around tiny dust particles to form clouds and fog. This is called condensation.
The temperature at which this occurs is called the dew point.

3. PRECIPITATION
When water droplets in clouds combine and grow heavy enough, they fall back to
Earth as precipitation. Precipitation can take the form of rain, snow, sleet,
or hail, depending on atmospheric temperature.

4. COLLECTION & RUNOFF
Precipitation that reaches the ground either flows over the surface as runoff
into rivers, lakes, and oceans, or is absorbed into the ground through a process
called infiltration, where it becomes groundwater stored in underground aquifers.

5. TRANSPIRATION
Plants absorb groundwater through their roots and release it into the atmosphere
through tiny pores in their leaves called stomata, completing the cycle.

KEY FACTS:
- About 97% of Earth's water is found in the oceans.
- Only 3% of Earth's water is freshwater, and most of it is locked in glaciers.
- The water cycle is essential for distributing heat and freshwater across the planet.
- Human activities such as deforestation and urbanisation can disrupt the water cycle.
